The hidden waves inside sequence models

A new result shows that a leading family of AI sequence models is mathematically the same thing as a ring of coupled oscillators, and that it computes by sending waves around that ring.

← All writing

Modern language and sequence models are famously hard to explain. We can watch what they output, but tracing why a particular input leads to a particular answer usually means poking at the model from the outside, one case at a time.

A recent paper by Shikder, Dey, and colleagues at Western University and the Fields Institute takes a different route. Instead of probing a trained model empirically, they derive, from first principles and on paper, an exact mathematical description of what one important class of these models actually computes, from input all the way to output.

The surprise is what that description turns out to be: a network of coupled oscillators on a ring, computing by passing traveling waves around it. Below you can play with the waves yourself.

01 / The bottleneckWhy not just use Transformers?

Transformers, the architecture behind most large language models, capture relationships between words using attention, comparing every token in a sequence against every other token. That's powerful, but it's expensive: the cost grows with the square of the sequence length. Double the context, quadruple the work.

State space models (SSMs) sidestep this. Rather than comparing all pairs of tokens, they push each input into a small internal network and let it reverberate. The network's evolving state quietly accumulates a memory of everything it has seen. Cost grows only linearly with sequence length, and on many benchmarks SSMs now match or beat Transformers.

SSMs also carry a second, quieter promise. Their internal dynamics are linear, and linear systems are mathematically solvable. That opens a door that attention slams shut: the possibility of actually solving the model rather than just observing it.

02 / The correspondenceAn SSM is a ring of oscillators

Here is the paper's central move. The authors had previously found an exact solution for a particular network of nonlinear oscillators arranged on a ring. They show that the recurrent core of a widely-used SSM, the diagonal model S4D, turns out after a change of variables to be the very same system.

The bridge is a clean piece of mathematics. Both systems advance their state by repeatedly applying a single operator. Writing the network's nodes in the right basis, the discrete Fourier modes of a ring, turns that operator diagonal, meaning each mode evolves completely independently of the others:

xk = eDτ xk−1 + B̅ uk state update: evolve, then inject the new input uk

Matching the two systems' operators term-for-term makes them equivalent. And that equivalence hands us something the SSM never made explicit on its own: a concrete physical picture. The abstract latent state becomes activity spread over a ring of nodes, and each independent mode becomes a wave circling that ring at its own speed.

The model's "memory" isn't stored in abstract numbers. It's stored as waves: the recent past written into the shape of activity circling the ring.

03 / Storing the pastEach input excites its own wave

Because every mode runs independently, an incoming signal gets split across them like light through a prism. A signal that wiggles at a particular rate resonates with the mode whose natural frequency matches it, exciting that wave strongly while leaving others quiet.

The authors test this directly. They train S4D to sort noisy signals into three buckets: ones hiding a 15 Hz tone, ones hiding a 20 Hz tone, and pure noise. The classes look nearly identical buried in the noise, but a single resonant mode lights up differently for each, and that's enough to tell them apart.

04 / The hard partWhat the nonlinearity does

Simple signals can be separated by wave energy alone. But real-world data, whether language, EEG, or sensor streams, isn't a clean tone. It smears across many modes at once, and pulling it apart needs more than reading off how loud each wave is.

That "more" comes from the nonlinear activation the model applies after its linear core (here, a function called GELU). Nonlinearities are exactly what makes end-to-end analysis hard: even if you fully understand the linear waves, the nonlinearity scrambles them in ways that resist clean description.

The authors crack this with a classical tool called Carleman embedding, which rewrites the nonlinearity as an infinite, but orderly, hierarchy of wave interactions:

r = 1

Single waves. The plain response: the waves each input excites on its own. This first order alone explains 83% of the model's accuracy on a real EEG task.

r = 2

Pairs of waves. The nonlinearity makes two waves interact, in a way that depends on their relative phase, not just their loudness. Adding this term lifts the explanation to 94%.

r ≥ 3

Many-wave interactions. Progressively finer corrections. Including the full activation reaches 100%, confirming the expansion converges to the real model.

The headline: most of what the model does is captured by waves and pairs of interacting waves. The crucial extra ingredient beyond simple resonance is phase: two waves carrying the same energy can cooperate or cancel depending on their alignment.

05 / The payoffOne equation for the whole forward pass

Putting it together, the paper writes a single closed-form operator that maps any input sequence to the model's output classification, the complete forward pass with linear core and nonlinearity included:

O = (1/T) · W · Σk Λ( ℜ( C [ Dτ xk + B̅ uk ] ) ) input uk → recurrence → mode mixing C → nonlinearity Λ → readout W → output O

This is the thing post-hoc interpretability can't give you: not a measurement of the trained model, but a derivation of it. In principle you can predict what the model will output without even running it, and read directly which waves and which wave-interactions drive each decision.

The forward pass shifts from something you probe empirically to something you can write down, term by term.

The authors note this generalizes across diagonal SSM variants, and point toward two payoffs. Design: tuning the connectivity could engineer longer-lived waves that hold longer-range dependencies. Control and safety: if you know the exact mapping from input to output, you might steer it deliberately, a kind of guarantee that's simply unavailable when interpretability rests on observation alone.

06 / In a sentenceThe takeaways

▷

A leading SSM (S4D) is exactly equivalent to a solvable ring of coupled oscillators.

▷

It stores the recent past as traveling waves circling that ring; different inputs excite different waves.

▷

Its nonlinearity makes waves interact by phase, and just single waves plus pairs explain 94% of real-task performance.

▷

The whole input-to-output map is one explicit equation, turning interpretability from observation into derivation.
