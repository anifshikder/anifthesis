Interpretability · State space models

The sequence model you can solve on paper

Most AI sequence models can only be explained after the fact. A new architecture called S¹ can be written down from input to output as a single exact equation, with no guesswork.

← All writing

When a model decides that an EEG trace belongs to one mental state or that a handwritten digit is a 7, we would like to know why. For most modern sequence models, the honest answer is that we don't, not exactly.

The usual tools, attention maps and gradient attributions, tell us which parts of the input mattered, but not how the model's internal machinery turned that input into that answer. A paper by Dey, Shikder, and colleagues at Western University and the Fields Institute takes a sharper route: they design an architecture, S¹, whose entire computation, dynamics and readout together, can be written as one closed-form equation derived from first principles.

The trick is to start not from a neural network, but from a network of coupled oscillators whose physics happens to be exactly solvable.

01 / The bottleneckWhy SSMs are almost transparent

State space models (SSMs) like S4 and Mamba have become a strong alternative to Transformers. Internally they have two parts: a linear recurrence that absorbs the input sequence and a nonlinear readout that converts the internal state into a prediction.

The linear half is wonderfully tractable. You can find its natural modes and write down its response to any input exactly. The trouble is the second half. SSMs need that nonlinear readout to perform well, but the nonlinearity severs the clean connection between the model's internal modes and its output. Once it's applied, end-to-end analysis falls apart.

So the challenge isn't to throw away the nonlinearity, the model can't work without it, but to find an architecture where the nonlinearity is part of the math rather than bolted on afterward.

02 / The constructionA readout that derives itself

Here's the central idea. S¹ is built from a system of complex oscillators on a ring. A specific change of coordinates turns the messy nonlinear oscillator dynamics into a simple linear system, the kind we can solve exactly with a matrix exponential.

The payoff is subtle but decisive: the inverse of that same coordinate change tells you precisely what the readout nonlinearity has to be. It turns out to be a clean operation, normalizing each node's state to unit length so only its phase survives:

readout = y / |y| = eiψ keep the phase ψ of each oscillator, discard the magnitude

This is the part that sets S¹ apart. In other SSMs the nonlinearity is a heuristic choice, an off-the-shelf activation. Here it is uniquely determined by the model's own structure. It isn't appended; it falls out of the mathematics.

Fixing the connectivity to a ring means every mode is, forever, a traveling wave of a known spatial frequency, no matter how training reshapes the rest of the model.

03 / Storing the inputEach mode is a tuned resonator

Because the ring's modes are the discrete Fourier modes, each one evolves completely independently, like a separate radio channel. An incoming signal gets split across these channels, and each channel acts as a resonator: it has a natural frequency and a decay rate, and it responds strongly only to inputs that match its tuning.

The authors show this directly on real data. On a slow-cortical-potential EEG task, the model's energy piles into the lowest-frequency modes, matching the slow nature of those brain signals, with the two classes differing in how strongly they drive those modes. On sequential MNIST, a digit 7 lights up middle modes while a digit 1, with its simple vertical stroke, stays in the lowest ones.

04 / The hard partThe nonlinearity is the wave interaction

Here is where S¹ turns the usual problem inside out. In a standard SSM the nonlinearity is opaque. In S¹, the phase-normalization readout has a precise spectral meaning: its denominator couples every mode to every other mode through their interference.

Concretely, the readout's magnitude term sums products of pairs of modes, each pair carrying a phase difference. When two modes are aligned they reinforce; when opposed they cancel, even if neither changed its energy. The nonlinearity, in other words, isn't an external activation at all: it is the mechanism by which the model reaches the phase dynamics of the original oscillator system.

step 1

Modal decomposition. The state is a superposition of traveling waves, each with its own amplitude that accumulates the input history through a frequency-tuned filter.

step 2

Inter-modal interference. Normalizing by magnitude couples the modes in pairs. Aligned phases add; opposed phases cancel. This is where the nonlinear work happens.

step 3

Linear readout. A learned matrix weights each node by its phase coherence and preferred direction to produce the class scores.

Interactive · What the readout sees

05 / The payoffOne operator for the whole forward pass

Stitching the pieces together, the paper writes a single closed-form operator that maps an input sequence to its class scores, with the modal decomposition, the inter-modal interference, and the readout all included explicitly:

Oc = Σn Wcn rn eiφn read out each node's phase coherence rn and mean direction φn

Reading this is the thing post-hoc methods can never give you: not a measurement of a trained model, but a derivation of it. You can trace, in closed form, how any input flows through identifiable wave modes to the final decision. The classification is no longer something you probe; it's something you can write down.

Every stage of the computation admits a closed-form account, with no post-hoc attribution required.

The same transparency carries respectable numbers. On the UEA multivariate time-series benchmarks S¹ sits in the same range as S5, Mamba and LRU, and it reaches 97% on sequential MNIST with a single layer. Full mathematical explainability, the authors argue, does not have to cost performance.

06 / SteeringFlipping a decision on purpose

Because the operator is exact, it lets the authors ask a causal question: do specific wave modes actually drive a decision? They freeze the readout and intervene directly on a handful of identifiable modes, amplifying, retuning, or re-driving them partway through a sequence.

The result is striking. On the EEG task, nudging just five modes by tiny amounts is enough to flip every sample from class 0 to class 1, a genuine change in the winning class rather than a cosmetic margin shift. Since the readout never moved, the flip is causally attributable to those modes alone, a finite, mechanistic recipe for redirecting the model.

07 / In a sentenceThe takeaways

▷

S¹ is built from an exactly solvable ring of complex oscillators, so its dynamics and its readout can be written down in closed form.

▷

Its nonlinearity isn't chosen, it's derived: the inverse coordinate change uniquely fixes it as phase normalization.

▷

The forward pass becomes one operator built from traveling-wave modes interacting by phase, turning interpretability from observation into derivation.

▷

The same math enables control: tiny pushes on a few modes can flip a classification on purpose, with full transparency, all while staying competitive on benchmarks.
